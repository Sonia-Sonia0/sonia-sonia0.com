Case study 01
Integra SCADA HMI Redesign
UGL · Product Designer · Desktop + Mobile · 2022–2025
Safety-critical Infrastructure Desktop + Mobile
Opening

When an infrastructure operator misses a critical alarm, the consequences aren't a bad user experience — they're a service failure, a safety risk, or worse. The Integra SCADA platform is the system those operators rely on, 24 hours a day, to monitor and control live infrastructure assets. By the time we completed the redesign, system testing showed a 25% improvement in emergency response time — the result of designing every screen around one question: what does an operator need to stay in control under pressure?

The problem

Operators worked in 24/7 control rooms with a legacy interface that created visual inconsistency, unreliable alarm hierarchy, and eye strain across long shifts. During contextual research, we observed operators scanning through dozens of simultaneous alarms during incidents — unable to quickly identify which required immediate action.

"When something goes wrong, everything goes red. I can't tell what's actually on fire."— Operator, contextual inquiry
Design decisions
01 · Dark theme UI — sustained attention, not preference

Operators work in low-ambient control rooms across 8–12 hour shifts. The legacy bright interface caused physical eye strain. A dark theme was an environmental decision, not aesthetic — validated against WCAG 2.1 AA under actual control room lighting. We ruled out a configurable toggle: in safety-critical environments, consistency across all operators matters more than personal preference.

02 · Structured alarm prioritisation — reducing noise to protect signal

We restructured the alarm panel around a three-tier model — Critical, Elevated, Informational — using visual weight rather than colour alone. Critical alarms occupy a persistent top zone that cannot be scrolled past. Priority-first won over grouped-by-asset because it matched how operators think in an emergency: severity first, then location.

03 · Modular components — consistency as a safety feature

The legacy interface had been built by different teams in isolation — the same data looked different across screens. We built a shared Figma component library with strict behavioural rules. Any component responds identically across every screen. Consistency here isn't a design preference — it's a safety consideration.

Outcomes
25%
Faster emergency response in system testing
WCAG AA
Accessibility compliance validated in situ
1 library
Unified component system for all future modules
Reflection

The most important thing I'd do differently: involve operators earlier. Access came later in the process than I would have chosen. I'd have fought harder for that access from day one. What this project taught me is that good design in complex environments is as much about what you remove as what you add.

Case study 02
Performance Management System
Transport for NSW · Sole Lead UX Designer at UGL · Web + Mobile · 2023–2025
Government 0→1 Product Cross-platform Enterprise
Opening

Managing Sydney's train fleet means making high-stakes decisions in real time — which units are available, which are in maintenance, which are delayed, and what that means for service contracts worth millions. The platform we delivered reduced reporting time by 50%, eliminated manual reconciliation across three teams, and gave operations controllers a live view of fleet availability for the first time. I was the sole lead designer at UGL delivering for Transport for NSW, responsible for 13+ screen types across web and mobile.

The problem

Before this platform, three separate teams — operations, maintenance, and finance — managed train performance data across disconnected spreadsheets and email threads. Operations controllers worked from information hours out of date. Maintenance planners lost job logs in handovers. Finance teams reconciled mismatched data after the fact.

"We're making decisions about the fleet based on yesterday's data. By the time the spreadsheet gets updated, the situation has already changed."— Operations controller, synthesised from stakeholder sessions
Design decisions
01 · Cross-platform — one system, two contexts

We didn't design desktop first and adapt for mobile. Maintenance staff in noisy depots needed to log a fault fast, one hand free — a categorically different context from an operations manager analysing fleet trends across multiple screens. Mobile was designed around the smallest unit of work; desktop around density and familiarity. Two platforms, one shared design system, deliberately distinct interaction models.

02 · Information hierarchy across 13+ screens

One principle applied consistently: status before detail, exceptions before routine, actions before information. Operations controllers see live status. Managers see aggregated performance. Finance sees contract-relevant data filtered for payment reconciliation. Same data, different lens — validated through stakeholder reviews with each team.

Outcomes
50%
Reduction in reporting time
3
User groups unified in one system
13+
Screen types across web & mobile
Reflection

The hardest part wasn't the complexity of the design problems — it was making consequential decisions in isolation as the sole designer. I got better at writing down the why before the what. It's a discipline I now carry into every project regardless of team size. With one more voice pressure-testing the work, it could have been even better.

Case study 03
Insurance Management App
Confidential Client · UX Designer · Web + iOS + Android
Fintech Onboarding iOS + Android + Web
Opening

Insurance is one of the most important financial decisions people make — and one of the least understood. The client had built a fully managed insurance advisory service and needed a digital product that made that relationship feel as trustworthy and simple as it actually was. My contribution focused on the onboarding and document signing flows: the make-or-break moment where a new user decides whether to trust us with their financial life.

The problem

The core tension: the information users need to give to get value is exactly the information they're most reluctant to hand over. New users had to verify identity, sign legal documents, and input existing contracts — all before experiencing a single benefit. Three user types, each arriving with different levels of existing trust.

"I don't understand what I'm signing. I just click through and hope for the best."— Insurance app user, representative of target audience sentiment
Design decisions
01 · Two-phase registration — reducing perceived burden

We split registration into two named phases with a natural pause point. Phase 1 framed around access. Phase 2 framed around value — personalised service, not more admin. For existing customers, data was pre-filled wherever possible. Users who completed Phase 1 had already invested; Phase 2 completion followed naturally.

02 · Document signing — transparent, not bureaucratic

Each document opened with a plain-language explanation — the first thing seen, not a footer footnote. Two signature patterns: drawn signatures for formal legal documents (deliberate, considered commitment); toggle switches for lower-stakes confirmations. The goal: a user who signs because they understand, not because they gave up trying to.

03 · Cross-platform consistency without losing platform conventions

Shared design system and interaction model across web, iOS, and Android — then adapted to each platform's native conventions. Web included a manual dark/light toggle; mobile followed system settings. Users' muscle memory for their platform was respected within a consistent visual and behavioural frame.

Outcomes
3
Platforms delivered (Web, iOS, Android)
2-phase
Onboarding reducing drop-off
10+
Service types supported at scale
Reflection

Working in a team, the onboarding flows I owned sat upstream of everything else. Every decision had downstream implications for screens another designer was building. Clear documentation of rationale wasn't a courtesy — it was a dependency. I'd do more user testing on the signing flow before build. Solving the trust problem matters more than solving the interaction problem.

How I work
01
Start with people

Before any wireframe, I need to understand who's using the system, under what conditions, and what's genuinely hard for them. Research informs every decision.

02
Design with reasoning

Every design decision has a why. I document the alternatives considered and the reason for the choice — not just for stakeholders, but to keep my own thinking honest.

03
Test and iterate

Assumptions get challenged early. Whether usability testing or stakeholder review, I'd rather find the friction in a prototype than in a live system.

04
Measure what changed

Design is only as good as the outcome it produces. I connect decisions to results — for users and for the business — not just to deliverables.
