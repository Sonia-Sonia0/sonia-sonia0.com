Case study 01
Integra SCADA HMI Redesign
UGL · Product Designer · Desktop + Mobile · 2022–2025
Safety-criticalInfrastructureDesktop + Mobile
Opening

When an infrastructure operator misses a critical alarm, the consequences aren't a bad user experience — they're a service failure, a safety risk, or worse. By the time we completed the redesign, system testing showed a 25% improvement in emergency response time — the result of designing every screen around one question: what does an operator need to stay in control under pressure?

The problem

Operators worked in 24/7 control rooms with a legacy interface causing visual inconsistency, unreliable alarm hierarchy, and eye strain across long shifts. We observed operators scanning through dozens of simultaneous alarms — unable to quickly identify which required immediate action.

"When something goes wrong, everything goes red. I can't tell what's actually on fire."

— Operator, contextual inquiry
Key design decisions
01 · Dark theme — sustained attention, not preference

Operators work in low-ambient control rooms across 8–12 hour shifts. A dark theme was an environmental decision, validated against WCAG 2.1 AA. We ruled out a configurable toggle — consistency across all operators matters more than personal preference in a safety-critical environment.

02 · Three-tier alarm prioritisation

We restructured alarms around Critical, Elevated, and Informational — using visual weight rather than colour alone. Critical alarms occupy a persistent top zone that cannot be scrolled past. Priority-first won because it matched how operators think in an emergency: severity first, then location.

03 · Modular components — consistency as a safety feature

We built a shared Figma component library with strict behavioural rules. Any component responds identically across every screen. Consistency here isn't a design preference — it's a safety consideration.

Outcomes
25%
Faster emergency response in system testing
WCAG AA
Accessibility compliance validated in situ
1 library
Unified component system for all modules
Reflection

The most important thing I'd do differently: involve operators earlier. I'd have fought harder for that access from day one. What this project taught me is that good design in complex environments is as much about what you remove as what you add.

Case study 02
Performance Management System
Transport for NSW · Sole Lead UX Designer · Web + Mobile · 2023–2025
Government0→1 ProductCross-platformEnterprise
Opening

Managing Sydney's train fleet means making high-stakes decisions in real time — against service contracts worth millions. The platform we delivered reduced reporting time by 50%, eliminated manual reconciliation across three teams, and gave operations controllers live fleet visibility for the first time. I was the sole lead designer at UGL delivering for Transport for NSW — responsible for 13+ screen types across web and mobile.

The problem

Three teams — operations, maintenance, finance — managed train performance data across disconnected spreadsheets. Controllers worked from hours-old information. Maintenance logs got lost in handovers. Finance reconciled mismatched data after the fact.

"We're making decisions about the fleet based on yesterday's data. By the time the spreadsheet gets updated, the situation has already changed."

— Operations controller, synthesised from stakeholder sessions
Key design decisions
01 · One system, two completely different contexts

We didn't design desktop first. Maintenance staff in noisy depots needed to log a fault fast, one hand free — a categorically different context from an operations manager analysing fleet trends. Mobile was designed around the smallest unit of work; desktop around density and insight.

02 · Information hierarchy across 13+ screen types

One principle applied consistently: status before detail, exceptions before routine, actions before information. Operations sees live status. Management sees performance trends. Finance sees contract-relevant data. Same data source, different lens.

Outcomes
50%
Reduction in reporting time
3
User groups unified in one platform
13+
Screen types across web & mobile
Reflection

The hardest part wasn't design complexity — it was making consequential decisions as the sole designer. I got better at writing the why before the what. With one more voice pressure-testing the work, it could have been even better.

Case study 03
Insurance Management App
Confidential Client · UX Designer · Web + iOS + Android
FintechOnboardingiOS + Android + Web
Opening

The client needed a digital product that made their fully managed insurance advisory service feel as trustworthy and simple as it actually was. My focus: the onboarding and document signing flows — the make-or-break moment where a new user decides whether to trust you with their financial life.

The problem

The core tension of all fintech design: the information users need to give to get value is exactly what they're most reluctant to hand over. Three user types, different levels of existing trust, one onboarding flow.

"I don't understand what I'm signing. I just click through and hope for the best."

— Insurance app user, representative sentiment
Key design decisions
01 · Two-phase registration

Phase 1 framed around access. Phase 2 framed around value. Users who completed Phase 1 were already invested; the second phase followed naturally. Existing customer data pre-filled wherever possible.

02 · Document signing — transparent, not bureaucratic

Plain-language explanation before every signature. Two signature types: drawn for formal legal documents, toggle for lower-stakes confirmations. The goal: a user who signs because they understand — not because they gave up.

03 · Cross-platform without losing native feel

Shared design system across web, iOS, and Android — adapted to each platform's native conventions. Users' muscle memory respected within a consistent visual frame.

Outcomes
3
Platforms (Web, iOS, Android)
2-phase
Onboarding reducing drop-off
10+
Service types supported
Reflection

Working in a team, my onboarding flows sat upstream of everything else. Clear documentation of rationale wasn't a courtesy — it was a dependency. Solving the trust problem matters more than solving the interaction problem.

How I work

My design process

01
Start with people

Before any wireframe, I need to understand who's using the system, under what conditions, and what's genuinely hard for them. Research informs every decision.

02
Design with reasoning

Every design decision has a why. I document alternatives considered and the reason for the choice — to keep my own thinking honest and handoffs clear.

03
Test and iterate

Assumptions get challenged early. Whether usability testing or stakeholder review, I'd rather find the friction in a prototype than in a live system.

04
Measure outcomes

Design is only as good as the outcome it produces. I connect decisions to results — for users and for the business — not just to deliverables.
